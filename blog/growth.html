Neuroplasticity and growth mindset

How neuroplasticity and growth mindset is relevant to learning

CULTURAL

written by Justin Cole

Dec 1 2020

Neuroplasticity is defined as the ability of the nervous system to change its activity in response to internal or external stimuli by reorganizing its structure, functions, or connections. It was once thought that the brain could only change in development but this has been disproven. Though the neuroplasticity of a brain in development is much greater than that of a fully formed brain, we all have the ability to reshape, develop and grow.

The ten principles of neuroplasticity, the factors identified as especially important in facilitating neuroplasticity.

  1. Use it or lose it
    Failure to drive specific brain functions can lead to loss of abilities.
  2. Use it and improve it
    Training that drives a specific brain function can lead to improving abilities.
  3. Specificity
    The nature of the training experience dictates the nature of the change in the brain.
  4. Repetition matter
    Change requires sufficient repetition.
  5. Intensity matters
    Change requires intensive training.
  6. Time matters
    Different forms of change in the brain happen at different times during training.
  7. Salience matters
    The training experience must be meaningful to the person in order to cause change.
  8. Age matters
    Training-induced change occurs more readily in younger brains.
  9. Transference
    Change in function as a result of one training experience can even lead to learning other similar skills.
  10. Interference
    Brain changes that result in bad habits can interfere with learning good habits.

By leveraging the knowledge of these principals to perform deliberate practice of meaningful and novel activities, you can effectively give your brains a workout. In doing so, making it stronger and more connected. these activities can include things like learning a new language, learning an instrument or mindfulness. If you can partake in activities and goals that immerse the motor, auditory and visual parts of your brain, you’re more likely to engage your corpus callosum. This’ll increase your bridged connections between your left brain and right brain, which means you’ll become better at solving complex problems and have better coordination.

Neuroplastic change - how to get started

Component How it works Try it now
Challenge & Novelty The skill should be new-to-you and not too easy to learn. Novelty and challenge are essential components for cognitive change. Think of something you’ve always wanted to try (e.g., learn a new language, mountain climbing, play the guitar).
Intention The skill you are practicing must have some meaning, relevance, or importance to you. Neuroplastic change is associated with the feeling of being rewarded while engaging in a new task. Think about why you want to learn this skill (e.g., I love traveling, I love being in the mountains, I love guitar music). Make sure it matters to you personally, then write it down.
Specific Attention The skills we practice tend to improve. Focusing on exact tasks increases the likelihood of change. Have a plan. Schedule time in your calendar to focus on this task. Consider setting a personal goal and incentive.
Repetition & Intensity One-and-done is not enough. Shorter bouts of intense repetition are usually needed to creating new connections. Check in with yourself weekly. Have you been following your plan? If not, adjust and keep going.
Time Be patient with yourself. Neuroplastic change takes time in order for structural and functional change to occur. Plan for lapses – it’s normal. Refocus, adjust your schedule, and keep going.

What is growth mindset?

Growth Mindset: “In a growth mindset, people believe that their most basic abilities can be developed through dedication and hard work. Brains and talent are just the starting point. This view creates a love of learning and a resilience that is essential for great accomplishment.” (Dweck, 2015)

What's surprising about this is most of our academic life has been based around a static or fixed mindset. That intelligence is a fixed, inborn and unchangeable quality.

For myself personally, I would like to integrate more of a growth mindset into my learning journey. I think it will be really important to realise that it isn't about not having the ability but not having the ability yet. Also, taking on challenges that are at the right level of difficulty for my ability. Something aligned but just a bit of a stretch, as to experience a challenge. By focusing on the process rather than the outcome, my efforts will lead to change, learning and growth both now and in the future.
